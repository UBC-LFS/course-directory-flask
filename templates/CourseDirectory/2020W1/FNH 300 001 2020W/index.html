Course Objectives

Food Engineering is the combined application of multiple sciences (including but not limited to physics, chemistry and mathematics) and engineering branches (including but not limited to mechanical engineering, chemical engineering and agricultural engineering) in order to understand and design operations in the food industry.

 

Bringing food to consumers that is safe, nutritious and delicious presents a number of technical challenges. Food Engineers serve humanity by providing the technical know-how required for transforming the agricultural produce into food, products and services. A good grasp over physics, chemistry and mathematics is a pre-requisite for understanding and designing these food, products and operations.

This course aims to provide an introduction to the fundamental scientific concepts and engineering skills needed to understand commercial food processes, as well as to design simple food systems. The first part of the course covers basic scientific concepts of units and dimensions, mass and energy balances, and thermodynamics. The second part of the courses covers basic fundamentals of transport phenomenon (fluid flow, heat transfer and mass transfer) required for designing unit operations in the food industry. Because of the practical need for quantitative prediction of process outcomes, topics are examined in mathematical as well as descriptive terms.

By the end of this course students should be able to do the following:

  • employ a systematic problem-solving method for addressing engineering questions, including making and testing assumptions
  • calculate mass and energy balances in the context of food processing equipment
  • describe the different modes of heat and momentum transfer and explain the effects of each on a food process of interest to the student
  • select appropriate mathematical models for a given situation and use them to calculate important food processing parameters such as sterilization time, heating/cooling rate, or flow rate
  • estimate the relative importance of different physical phenomena from dimensionless numbers
  • know where to look to find information about food engineering principles and processing equipment

Breakdown of Course Content

  1. Engineering Principles and Mass Balances (∼3 lectures)
    • Engineering units and conversions
    • Engineering problem-solving methodology
      • assumptions, approximations, and heuristics
      • mathematical models
    • Conservation of Mass
      • control volume
    • Steady-state and transient mass balances

2.   Energy Balances & Thermodynamics (∼4 lectures)

  • Conservation of energy (1st Law of Thermodynamics)
    • Types of energy and work
  • Thermodynamic properties of materials
    • extensive and intensive properties
    • heat capacity, latent heat, enthalpy
    • steam tables
  • Equations of state
    • vapor-liquid equilibrium
  • Steady-state and transient energy balances
  • Economic considerations for energy usage

3.   Fluid Flow (∼4 lectures)

  • Pumps and flow meters
    • liquids and gases
  • Viscosity and Reynolds number
    • Laminar vs. turbulent flow
  • Bernoulli equation
    • work, kinetic energy, potential energy, pressure gradient, frictional losses
    • pipe sizing
  • Rheology and granular materials

4.   Heat Transfer (∼5 lectures)

  • Conduction
    • thermal conductivity, thermal diffusivity
  • Convection
    • heat transfer coefficient
    • Prandtl number, Nusselt number,
    • forced vs. natural convection
  • Radiation
    • electromagnetic spectrum, black body
  • Steady-state heat transfer
    • heat exchangers
  • Transient heat transfer
  • Sterilization (D-values and Z-values)

5.   Group Presentation Example Topics (∼4 lectures)

  • Unit Operations in Food Science
  • Mass transfer Fundamentals
  • Distillation
  • Adsorption
  • Membrane Separation
  • Dehydration
  • Separation
  • Extrusion
  • Computational Fluid Dynamics
  • Refrigeration
  • Evaporation
  • Process control techniques
  • Packaging Principles
  • Any other topic approved by Instructor
