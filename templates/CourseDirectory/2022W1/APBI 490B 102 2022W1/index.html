COURSE DESCRIPTION

In this course we will explore the fascinating relationship between flowering plants and their animal pollinators. Through lectures, readings in the primary literature, writing, in-class discussions and lab/field activities, students will gain an appreciation for and aquire a basis of knowledge about the evidence and inquiry within this dynamic field of study.

LEARNING OUTCOMES

  1. Gain a deeper understanding of how science and scientists work
  2. Demonstrate basic knowledge of the biology (especially ecology, evolution) of plants and their pollinators
  1. Describe factors contributing to decline, potential remediation, knowledge gaps, policies impacting pollination in world agro-ecosystems
  2. Demonstrate knowledge on basic bee cognition, findings in cognitive ecology relevant to bees
  3. Demonstrate knowledge on common bee species, including honeybees, and their ecology
  4. Collect and analyze data on biodiversity
  5. Identify the pollination system and role of wild/honeybees in sustainable food systems

LEARNING ACTIVITIES

  1. Share/discuss prepared and spontaneous ideas in a course setting (required)
  2. Learn and practice basic field pollination ecology skills (required)
  3. Present data from a field activity in the form of a basic report of findings (required)
  4. Learn the basic ecology and science of pollination ecology and its relevance to conversation and policy (required)
  5. Learn basic North American native bee genera, be able to describe basic bee ecology and habits (required)

Statement regarding online learning for international students during the COVID pandemic

During this pandemic, the shift to online learning has greatly altered teaching and studying at UBC, including changes to health and safety considerations. Keep in mind that some UBC courses might cover topics that are censored or considered illegal by non-Canadian governments. This may include, but is not limited to, human rights, representative government, defamation, obscenity, gender or sexuality, and historical or current geopolitical controversies. If you are a student living abroad, you will be subject to the laws of your local jurisdiction, and your local authorities might limit your access to course material or take punitive action against you. UBC is strongly committed to academic freedom, but has no control over foreign authorities (please visit http://www.calendar.ubc.ca/vancouver/index.cfm?tree=3,33,86,0 for an articulation of the values of the University conveyed in the Senate Statement on Academic Freedom).  Thus, we recognize that students will have legitimate reason to exercise caution in studying certain subjects. If you have concerns regarding your personal situation, consider postponing taking a course with manifest risks, until you are back on campus or reach out to your academic advisor to find substitute courses. For further information and support, please visit: http://academic.ubc.ca/supportresources/freedom-expression.
